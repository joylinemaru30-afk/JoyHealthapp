Prevention of Typhoid

What is Typhoid?

Typhoid fever is a serious bacterial infection caused by Salmonella typhi. It spreads through contaminated food, water, or contact with an infected person. Typhoid primarily affects developing regions with poor sanitation and limited access to clean water.

Did you know? Each year, over 11–20 million people are affected by typhoid worldwide, leading to approximately 128,000–161,000 deaths (World Health Organization).

Symptoms and Transmission

Recognizing the symptoms early helps prevent complications and reduces the spread of the infection.

  • High fever (up to 104°F or 40°C)
  • Weakness, fatigue, or general malaise
  • Headache and abdominal pain
  • Loss of appetite and nausea
  • Constipation or diarrhea
  • Rash of flat, rose-colored spots

Transmission: Typhoid spreads through the fecal-oral route — when people consume food or water contaminated with feces from an infected person.

Preventive Measures

Typhoid prevention relies on maintaining good hygiene, ensuring safe food and water, and getting vaccinated. Below are key practices:

  • 1. Drink Safe Water: Use boiled, filtered, or bottled water. Avoid untreated or unfiltered water from rivers, wells, or streams.
  • 2. Maintain Sanitation: Keep toilets and surroundings clean. Properly dispose of human waste to prevent contamination.
  • 3. Wash Hands Frequently: Always wash hands with soap and clean water before eating or preparing food and after using the toilet.
  • 4. Eat Hygienic Food: Consume freshly cooked meals. Avoid raw vegetables and fruits unless they can be peeled safely.
  • 5. Avoid Street Food: Stay away from food sold in unhygienic conditions or open-air stalls.
  • 6. Get Vaccinated: Typhoid vaccination provides effective protection and is recommended for travelers to endemic regions.
  • 7. Proper Waste Disposal: Dispose of household waste responsibly to minimize the risk of contamination.
  • 8. Community Involvement: Encourage cleanliness drives and public awareness campaigns about safe hygiene and sanitation.

Vaccination Information

Vaccination is one of the most reliable ways to prevent typhoid. Two main types of vaccines are available:

  • Typhoid Conjugate Vaccine (TCV): Provides long-term protection and is suitable for children above 6 months of age.
  • Typhoid Vi Polysaccharide Vaccine: Usually given as an injection for individuals aged 2 years and older.

Consult your healthcare provider to determine the best vaccination option and schedule for you and your family.

Aftercare and Recovery

People recovering from typhoid should take extra care to prevent relapse and avoid spreading the infection:

  • Continue prescribed antibiotics until the full course is complete.
  • Rest adequately and maintain proper hydration with safe fluids.
  • Eat soft, digestible foods to support recovery.
  • Practice good hygiene to prevent reinfection or transmission.

Remember: Even after recovery, some individuals can still carry Salmonella typhi. Always follow your doctor’s advice for follow-up tests.
